Private Note: They compare the evolution of dwarves with M_*~10^6-10^7 M_sun (M_total ~10^10M_sun) using hydro+N_body simulations of both CDM and WDM (2keV). The star formation in the model including WDM is both reduced and delayed (by 1-2 Gyr) compared to that in the CDM universe, i.e. [very] effective on the MSP. However, the central density is reduced due to 'dynamical heating' depending on the SF efficiency, but independent on the DM particle, i.e. CCP is purely baryon-dependent and carries no infor about the nature of DM. They also investigate the distribution of stars with different ages that are affected by dynamical heating. This shows younger stars are more centrally concentrated, which is consistent with observations of stellar population gradients in nearby dwarves. **A paper worth reading carefully!**

Notes on the Missing Satellites Problem
Private Note: "Both kinematic studies and completeness-correction studies seem to point to a characteristic potential well depth for satellite subhalos that is quite close to the mass scale where photoionization and atomic cooling should limit galaxy formation." The scale where MSP starts arising is ~50kpc and lower. According to our theory of structure formation, lowmass halos collapse at high z, when the density of the Universe is higher, therefore they have a high central densityh concentration, which at the time of merging into more massive halos, help them resist tidal stripping in the center. and is the reason why some of them survive this process. The prediction for satellites of the MW is ~100 bound subhalos massive enough to host observable satellite galaxies with L>10^6L_sun. Fig 1.1 plots the abundance matching relation for a range of halo masses, indicating that the starformation efficiency peaks for M_h ~ 1e12 M_sun and decreases drastically for halos of both higher and lower masses. Subhalos (unlike halos) are not naturally characterized by a virial mass M_h. They tend to be truncated by tidal forces to radii smaller than their virial radii. the most common way to characteize a subhalo is to use V_max, the maximum value of the circular velocity curve v^2_c(r)=GM(r)/r. The radius at which V_max happens (r_max) depends on, is tipically set by tidal stripping. The other way is to assign a mass directly to the subhalo, either by defining the mass of the subhalo where the density reaches the mean background density of the host halo, estimating a tidal radius iteratively, or removing unbound particles from the subhalo iteratively until a boundary emerges from bound particles. MW-size halo has V_h~140 km/s. Uses Fig. 1.2 to argue that even though the total lnumber of CDM subhalos in N-body simulations is larger than MW's known satellites by orders of magnitude, one must remember that we _know_ that there should be a cutoff mass to galaxy formation, we just don'r know what that mass is, and Tollerud+2008's results shown in the upper blue dashed line in this figure (MS satellite abundance extrapolated from completeness correction of SDSS ultrafaint satellites) is promissing for LCDM, as it coincides with predicted abundance from simulations. Also mentions that different physical processes impose a different relevant mass scale; SN feedback V_max~100km/s, heating from photoionization V_max~30km/s, gas cooling ability (V_max~15km/s for Lya, and V_max~5km/s for H_2).
